Backrooms (2026) Review | 4.5/5

Parsons masterfully interwines lore and thematic messaging in his impressive directorial debut.

By Justin · June 1, 2026

The most memorable horror movies in recent memory have largely delivered on one key concept: grounding the plot in plausible, real-life experiences that are truly unnerving to the viewer. Hereditary centers around the slow desmise of family relationships following the death of a daughter, Get Out calls out unpsoken racial tensions that still underpin American culture, and Nope¹ challenges the idea that humans have (or can) truly gained control on the environment they live in. They bring uncomfortable ideas to the forefrunt of our minds, embody them into traditional horror elements, and offer an explanation on how we should face these ideas through the character’s interactions with these elements. All that to say, the true horror lies within the themes that underpin the movie, not the jump scares and unsettling monsters².

Going into the Backrooms, I was most curious on what themes Kane Parsons’ would decide to ground his debut film in — a daunting challenge given the ambiguity surrounding the concept of the Backrooms themselves. With a runtime of 110 minutes, it seemed nearly impossible to provide audiences with a thorough character exposition, explore the question of what the Backrooms are, and interwine a meaningful theme throughout the plot. One needed to be sacrificed in order to fully develop the others, and Parsons decides to lean less into the Backrooms themselves in order to thoroughly flesh out the characters and messaging of the movie — something I applaud him for doing, since I’d assume most other directors would choose to lean fully into the lore of the Backrooms, leaving character/theme development on the backburner. But Parsons’ dedication to the more “uninteresting” components of a horror movie is what makes the Backrooms one of the more profound entries in the genre.

The Walls We Build

The first third of the Backrooms is uninterested with the rooms themselves, instead allowing audiences to get a full understanding of film’s main character, Clark, whose internal turmoil will be used as the vehicle to convey thematic messaging throughout the entire film. While sitting in on one of Clark’s therapy sessions, we learn several key things about his character: 1) Clark’s marriage dissolved as he dedicated an increasing amount of time to his failing furniture store, 2) Clark believes the demise of his marriage was not his fault — it was a product of his wife’s lack of appreciation for his commitment to the furniture store / willingness to support her through law school (which clearly weighed on the couple’s financial stability), 3) Clark holds significant resentment against his wife for criticizing his devotion to work (and beers) despite making minimal financial contributions in their relationship. This is accentuated by a clarifying remark Clark makes during the first role-playing scene: Dr. Mary Cline (his therapist) says something like “let’s talk about night you were kicked out of the home”, and Clark proceeds to say “you mean the night I was kicked out of MY home. He also grows increasingly aggravated when Dr. Cline becomes more confrontational with Clark — specifically around the idea that being a the financial provider does not give you immunity from criticism by your significant others, nor an excuse to behave in any way you see fit. The 3 observations help us get a full understanding of Clark, the most important being his clear denial on why the marriage fell apart. Dr. Cline attempts to show him the role he played — the constant drinking, inattentiveness to his wife, choosing to start the ailing furniture store (which probably put an equal amount financial pressure on the relationship as the wife’s law school bills) — but Clark refuses to see it from her angle, grounding his innocence in the fact that he was the sole financial provider in the relationship. His memory of the relationship is wholly focused on this aspect. How could he be at fault for the relationship falling apart when he was the only one keeping a roof over their head and food on the table to begin with? It’s obvious to outside viewers, both Dr. Cline and us viewers, that Clark is a bit delusional — but Clark’s misremembrance of the past, what the day-to-day dynamics of the relationship were really like, build a misconstrued maze of memories leading him to believe he is fully innocent. For a man who shaped his life around furinture, physical boundaries in the real world, it’s ironic that Clark has constructed walls in his mind to protect himself from the truth.

We then move into the second act of the movie, Clark’s discovery of the Backrooms. A seemingly limitless space, clearly not made by human hands but with significant human influences imprinted everywhere you look. When asked to describe the rooms, Clark states “it’s like if you described a dog to someone, then asked them to draw it”. There would be semblances of a dog in the drawing, but anyone who’d actually seen a dog would be able to tell the depiction is clearly not one. Then, imagine if the process was repeated using the drawing of the dog — again, we’d get something that vaguely represents an animal, but this time even further from what it actually looks like. Parsons’ interpretation of the Backrooms largely parallels Clark’s explanation, but instead of dogs, the focus is on our memories. Throughout the film, Parsons communicates our misremembrance of the past can pervert our beliefs in the present — sometimes to such a degree that what we believe to be true is not the truth at all³, just like how the final drawing of the dog is in fact, not really a dog at all. This is most evidently displayed through Clark’s character, whose selective memory of the past leads him to become thoroughly convinced his failed marriage was no fault of his own. The misplaced anger and resentment leads to an isolated, depressing life for Clark as he is unable to reconcile and move on from the past

Like all great horror films, Parsons also embodies the theme through the Backrooms themselves. Here are the parallels we see between the Backrooms and Parsons’ central theme:

1) The Backrooms are an imperfect imitation of the human world, seemingly based on memories from people across the globe. The elements are strikingly human (street signs, chairs, and even pools), but the arrangement and proportions are clearly not. Any outsider would immediately recognize that these rooms are not from our world, but to whatever made these Rooms, it is an accurate replica. Thematically, Parsons frames the Rooms as a personification of our memories. To us, they are an accurate reflection of past events, but to an outsider, our memory could be severely flawed. It may resemble what happened in the past, but is distinctly not what transpired.

2) The “deeper” one goes into the Backrooms, the more inhuman they become. Think of the shot toward the end of the movie, where each layer of the Dr. Cline’s home strayed further from what the actual home looked like. In reality, the more time passes, the more our memories are prone to being misremembered. And as we continue to pull on inaccurate memories, our recollection of certain events stray further from the truth, and at some point, they don’t reflect what really happened at all. This is especially relevant for traumatic and damaging experiences we’ve been through (i.e Clark’s failed marriage).

3) The Backrooms are a seemingly endless loop. Pulling from Parsons Youtube series, we see instances where individuals walk for hours just to end up in the same spot they were before. It’s a confusing, entangled mess of infinite rooms, impossible for anyone to fully navigate and understand. Our imperfect memories feed us self-reinforcing loops of lies about the past, and the longer we let these lies persist, the harder it is to find our way out. If left unchecked, the web of delusion grows until it becomes impossible for us to navigate back to the truth.

Through the Backrooms, Parsons’ posits a terrifying question to the audience: What are you misremembering from the past? How has that deluded your present? And what can be done before you become hopelessly lost in maze of lies?⁴

Finding A Way Out

To navigate our endless trove of false memories, the film offers Dr. Mary Cline’s story as the roadmap out of the maze. Despite her own experience with trauma — namely her abusive, and likely schizophrenic mother that terrorized her childhood — Dr. Cline is able to “break “ the walls of her false memories through a brutally honest confrontation of the past.

We are given an intimate look at Mary’s childhood throughout the film, with Parsons first showing us a fond memory (Mary and her mother leaving handprints on the fresh concrete outside their new home) juxtaposed with a far more traumatic depiction (her mother keeping a young Mary captive inside the home, scarred that “they” are looking for the two) later on in the film. I believe the order in which these memories are presented alludes to the way an adult Mary recounts her childhood, with the happy memories bubbling to the surface as more sinister ones lie beneath, willfully ignored. This is further reinforced by how Mary covets the concrete imprint — the one piece of evidence that her childhood was not as devasting as what the truth suggests.

Like Clark, Mary’s misremembrance of the past wields a negative influence over her current life, most clearly manifested through the glimpses of Mary’s isolated lifestyle we see throughout the movie. Her home is dimly lit, and Parsons’ utilization of muted colors throughout the home (tan, brown are most prominent) imply a degree of depression plaguing Dr. Cline’s conscience. She is rarely shown interacting with anyone⁵ outside of her patients, with viewers led to believe Mary lacks a significant other and/ or social circle outside of her work life. She feels oddly content with such a monotonous existence, as her criticisms of Clark’s lack of friends suggests Mary would be aware enough to observe the same about her lifestyle. However, it’s implied that Mary’s failure to properly remember the past is directly obstructing her current view of life, preventing her from realizing the damage the past is wrecking on her present. It’s reasonable to assume that a traumatic relationship with one’s mother would impede a person’s ability to develop close bonds — whether it be friends or a significant other — out of the fear of abandonment and abuse that a loved one caused. Mary’s inability to reconcile with her past, instead replacing it with fleeting, pleasant impressions of her mother, destroys her ability to become close with anyone in her life.

Mary is pulled to the forefront of the plot when she’s dropped into the Backrooms in search of Clark. After being captured, Dr. Cline berates Clark for assigning blame to everybody but himself for past misfortunes, bringing his true memories to the surface and destroying the walls built up in his mind. At the same time, it appears Mary comes to a similar realization about her own past, that her mother, and her childhood, were far more sinister⁶ and damaging than what she wanted to believe. This is evidenced by the preceding scene, which shows the walls of Mary’s claustrophobic, confined home being bulldozed in — an allusion to the breaking down of walls in Mary’s mind. We also see Mary destroy the concrete imprint when fighting Pirate Clark, symbolic of Mary breaking free of her misconstrued memories to find her way back to reality — or in the movie, to “escape” the Backrooms.

While Mary’s character ultimately has a sad ending⁷, she appears to be only person we see successfully “escape” the Backrooms, and not-so-coincidentally the only character to rectify her past memories before it was too late. Through Mary’s journey, Parsons answers the question of what can be done before we are lost in the maze of lies from our past memories — breaking down the walls built in your mind, opening yourself to honest, uncomfortable self-criticism that gets you one step closer to the truth.

P.S: The film contains some of the best found footage horror scenes I’ve watched since the Blair Witch Project — the top 3 being the open scene (great homage to the OG Backrooms fans!), Bobby being pulled into the abyss by some unknown entity, and Clark being chased through the rooms (shoutout pool room!) by another unknown entity / the camera being picked up by said entity with Kat screaming “behind you!!!!” before the screen cuts to black. This, combined with the intrigue and mystery surrounding the Backrooms, is ultimately what makes the movie so good and rewatchable. I am also overwhelming underqualified to discuss any camera work / cinematography that makes the traditional horror elements of the Backrooms so good, so I won’t.
